## DECK 1: Legal Systems Foundations

### Card 1.1
**Q:** What are the two major legal traditions in the world?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Common Law** (England, UK, USA, Commonwealth) and **Civil Law** (Continental Europe, most of world)
</details>

---

### Card 1.2
**Q:** What is the main basis of Common Law?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Judicial precedent** - decisions made by judges in prior cases that bind future similar cases (stare decisis)
</details>

---

### Card 1.3
**Q:** What is the main basis of Civil Law?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Codified statutes** - comprehensive written legal codes (like the Napoleonic Code)
</details>

---

### Card 1.4
**Q:** What is the Corpus Iuris Civilis and when was it created?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

A compilation of Roman law ordered by **Emperor Justinian** in the **6th century CE**. It became the foundation of civil law systems.
</details>

---

### Card 1.5
**Q:** What is habeas corpus?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

A writ protecting individuals from **unlawful detention** - the right to be brought before a court to determine if detention is lawful.
</details>

---

### Card 1.6
**Q:** What is the Magna Carta and why is it important?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The "Great Charter" of **1215** - established that even the king is subject to law and guaranteed the right to **trial by jury**.
</details>

---

### Card 1.7
**Q:** Who wrote "Commentaries on the Laws of England" and why is it significant?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**William Blackstone** (1765-1769). It was the first comprehensive treatise on English common law and became the foundation for American law.
</details>

---

## DECK 2: EU Law Sources

### Card 2.1
**Q:** What is the hierarchy of EU law sources (top to bottom)?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

1. **International Law** (treaties)
2. **Primary EU Law** (TEU, TFEU, Charter)
3. **Secondary EU Law** (Regulations, Directives, Decisions)
4. **Soft Law** (Recommendations, Guidelines)
</details>

---

### Card 2.2
**Q:** What are the three binding forms of secondary EU law?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Regulations** - directly applicable, binding in entirety
2. **Directives** - binding as to result, must be transposed
3. **Decisions** - binding on specific addressees
</details>

---

### Card 2.3
**Q:** What is the key difference between a Regulation and a Directive?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

- **Regulation**: Directly applicable, no transposition needed, binding in entirety
- **Directive**: Must be transposed into national law, member states choose method, binding only as to results
</details>

---

### Card 2.4
**Q:** What happens if a member state fails to transpose a directive?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The **Commission** can bring **infringement proceedings** under **Article 258 TFEU** before the ECJ.
</details>

---

### Card 2.5
**Q:** What are Recommendations in EU law?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Non-binding** acts that suggest a course of action. They can influence policy but do not create legal obligations.
</details>

---

### Card 2.6
**Q:** Name 5 General Principles of EU Law
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

1. Proportionality
2. Legal certainty
3. Equal treatment
4. Legitimate expectations
5. Fundamental rights
</details>

---

## DECK 3: Competition Law

### Card 3.1
**Q:** What does Article 101 TFEU prohibit?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Anticompetitive agreements** between undertakings, decisions by associations, and concerted practices that affect trade and restrict competition.
</details>

---

### Card 3.2
**Q:** What are the 5 main types of anticompetitive agreements (Article 101)?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Price fixing**
2. **Output limitation**
3. **Market sharing**
4. **Bid rigging**
5. **Information exchange**

Memory: **POMBI**
</details>

---

### Card 3.3
**Q:** What does Article 102 TFEU prohibit?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Abuse of a dominant position** in the internal market or a substantial part of it.
</details>

---

### Card 3.4
**Q:** What market share typically indicates dominance under Article 102?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Generally **40% or more** market share creates a presumption of dominance (though other factors matter too).
</details>

---

### Card 3.5
**Q:** Name 5 types of abuse under Article 102
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

1. Excessive pricing
2. Predatory pricing
3. Margin squeezing
4. Refusal to deal
5. Tying and bundling
</details>

---

### Card 3.6
**Q:** What is margin squeezing?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

When a vertically integrated dominant firm sets the price of an input so high (or final product so low) that competitors cannot survive in the downstream market.
</details>

---

### Card 3.7
**Q:** What is a cartel?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

A secret agreement between competitors to fix prices, divide markets, limit output, or rig bids - the most serious antitrust violation.
</details>

---

### Card 3.8
**Q:** How do algorithms affect cartels in the digital era?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Algorithms can facilitate **tacit collusion** through:
- Price monitoring
- Automatic price matching
- Hub-and-spoke coordination via platforms
</details>

## DECK 4: Intellectual Property

### Card 4.1
**Q:** What does copyright protect?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Original **expressions** of ideas in literary, artistic, musical, and scientific works - NOT the ideas themselves.
</details>

---

### Card 4.2
**Q:** Is registration required for copyright protection in the EU?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**No** - copyright protection is **automatic** upon creation of the work. No formalities required.
</details>

---

### Card 4.3
**Q:** How long does copyright last in the EU?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Life of the author + 70 years** after death.
</details>

---

### Card 4.4
**Q:** What are the two types of rights in copyright?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Economic rights** - reproduction, distribution, communication to public (transferable)
2. **Moral rights** - attribution, integrity (usually non-transferable)
</details>

---

### Card 4.5
**Q:** What are neighboring (related) rights?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Rights protecting **performers, producers, and broadcasters** - related to copyright but with a different subject matter.
</details>

---

### Card 4.6
**Q:** What is the public domain?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Works where copyright has **expired** or that were never copyrightable - freely usable without permission.
</details>

---

### Card 4.7
**Q:** What is a copyright license?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Permission from the copyright holder to use the work under specified conditions. Can be **exclusive** or **non-exclusive**.
</details>

---

## DECK 5: Digital Regulation

### Card 5.1
**Q:** What does DSA stand for and what is its purpose?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Digital Services Act** - creates a safer, more accountable digital space by setting obligations for online platforms.
</details>

---

### Card 5.2
**Q:** What is a VLOP?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Very Large Online Platform** - platforms with **45 million+ monthly active users** in the EU (e.g., Instagram, TikTok, YouTube).
</details>

---

### Card 5.3
**Q:** What is the maximum fine under the DSA?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Up to **6% of global annual turnover**.
</details>

---

### Card 5.4
**Q:** Can platforms show targeted ads to minors under the DSA?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**No** - profiling-based advertising to minors is **prohibited** (Article 28).
</details>

---

### Card 5.5
**Q:** What are "dark patterns" and are they allowed under DSA?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Deceptive interface designs that trick users into actions they wouldn't normally take. They are **prohibited** under the DSA.
</details>

---

### Card 5.6
**Q:** What is the AI Act's risk-based approach?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Four risk levels:
1. **Unacceptable** (banned) - social scoring
2. **High risk** - strict requirements
3. **Limited risk** - transparency obligations
4. **Minimal risk** - free use
</details>

---

### Card 5.7
**Q:** What is an AI sandbox?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

A **controlled regulatory environment** allowing companies to test AI innovations under supervision before full deployment.
</details>

## DECK 6: Business Structures

### Card 6.1
**Q:** What are the key features of a Joint-Stock Company (JSC)?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

- Limited liability
- Share capital divided into transferable shares
- Separate legal personality
- Professional management (board structure)
</details>

---

### Card 6.2
**Q:** What were the key legal issues in the Uber case?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Labor law**: Are drivers employees or independent contractors?
2. **Licensing**: Is Uber a transport service (regulated) or information service?
</details>

---

### Card 6.3
**Q:** What is "limited liability"?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Shareholders are only liable up to the **amount they invested** - personal assets are protected from company debts.
</details>

## BONUS: Key Articles Quick Reference

### Card B.1
**Q:** What does Article 288 TFEU cover?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The **types of EU legal acts** (regulations, directives, decisions, recommendations, opinions).
</details>

---

### Card B.2
**Q:** What does Article 267 TFEU cover?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Preliminary references** - national courts asking the ECJ to interpret EU law.
</details>

---

### Card B.3
**Q:** What is the ordinary legislative procedure (Art. 294)?
<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The standard procedure: **Commission proposes** legislation adopted jointly by **Parliament and Council**.
</details>
